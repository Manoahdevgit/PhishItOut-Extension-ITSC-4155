How to Spot a Phishing Site

Phishing attacks have gotten a lot more convincing. Here's what to watch for.

🔗

Check the URL first

Attackers register domains that look almost identical to real ones — "paypa1.com" instead of "paypal.com", or "amazon-support.net" instead of "amazon.com". Always read the full address bar before entering anything.

🔒

HTTPS isn't a guarantee

A padlock means the connection is encrypted, not that the site is trustworthy. Plenty of phishing sites use HTTPS too. It's a good sign to look for, but don't rely on it alone.

⚠️

Urgency is a red flag

If a page is telling you your account will be locked, you've won something, or you need to act right now — slow down. Pressure tactics are one of the most common tricks in phishing.

📧

Don't click links in emails

Most phishing starts with an email. Instead of clicking the link they give you, open a new tab and type the website address yourself. Takes five extra seconds and protects your account.

🖥️

Sloppy design is a tell

Real companies care about how their sites look. Blurry logos, mismatched fonts, broken layouts, and spelling mistakes are signs someone threw the page together in a hurry.
